Definite article declension

Nominative þe þet se þo
Accusative þone þet þo þo
Dative þun þun þer þon
Genitive þes þes þer þor

This is based on a mixture of the OE article system and native palatal developments in Middle English.


Indefinite Article declension

A and An are used today exclusively as the indefinite article, coming from Old English an. What was forgotten in Varad's Reactionary Grammar section (Sticky II of the Ewbook 2.0) is that "an" is in fact New English "one". Perhaps due to Norman influence, the formation of an → one in early ME became mixed with French on and as a result the older and newer form of the word split. This is further supported by how an was used in Old English as well as fellow West Germanic languages today (Dutch een and German ein) where "one" is the indefinite article and also "a particular" (i.e OE an of ðam → one of those). In a way, this means that our current understanding that "one" meaning "a particular" being from French influence is misconstrued. What's more likely is the underdevelopment of a/an is a result of French influence instead.

Nominative one one one
Accusative onen onen onen
Dative one one oner
Genitive ones ones oner
Instrumental one one oner

This chart is based on the one above -- a mixture of the pre-Norman declension and how the pronunciation shifts by New English (based on comparisons).


Examples

Now some example sentences with the above grammar in WSE:

  • He spurn onen stone. (He kicked a stone)
  • Se girl and oner freend gede to þet learninghuse. (The girl and a friend of hers went to the school)
  • Ic slayed þe/þet shuck wiþ þon holy mece. (I slayed the demon with a holy sword)
